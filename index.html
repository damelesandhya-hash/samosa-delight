Authentic Samosa Recipe

Crispy, golden triangles filled with spiced potatoes and peas

45 mins 4 servings Easy

The Perfect Indian Snack

Samosas are a beloved snack across India and beyond. These crispy, triangular pastries are filled with spiced potatoes, peas, and sometimes meat, making them the perfect tea-time treat or party appetizer.

Ingredients

For the Dough:

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour
  • 4 tbsp ghee or oil
  • 1 tsp salt
  • Water as needed

For the Filling:

  • 3 large potatoes, boiled and mashed
  • 1 cup green peas
  • 1 tbsp ginger-garlic paste
  • 2 green chilies, finely chopped
  • 1 tsp cumin seeds
  • 1 tsp garam masala
  • Salt to taste
  • Oil for frying

Instructions

1

Make the Dough

Mix flour, salt, and ghee. Add water gradually to form a stiff dough. Cover and rest for 30 minutes.

2

Prepare Filling

Heat oil, add cumin seeds. Add ginger-garlic paste, chilies, then potatoes and peas. Add spices and cook for 5 minutes.

3

Shape Samosas

Divide dough into balls, roll into circles, cut in half. Form cones, fill with mixture, and seal edges.

4

Fry to Perfection

Heat oil to medium heat. Fry samosas until golden brown and crispy. Drain on paper towels.

5

Serve Hot

Serve with mint chutney, tamarind sauce, or your favorite dip. Best enjoyed fresh and hot!

Pro Tips

Oil Temperature

Maintain medium heat (350°F/175°C) for perfect crispy texture without burning.

Proper Sealing

Use a flour-water paste to seal edges tightly to prevent filling from leaking during frying.

Dough Resting

Let the dough rest for at least 30 minutes for easier rolling and better texture.

Cultural Heritage

Origins & History

Samosas have a rich history dating back to the Middle East, where they were known as "sambosa." They traveled to India via Central Asian traders and became an integral part of Indian cuisine. Today, they're enjoyed worldwide as a popular street food and appetizer.

Regional Variations

Different regions have their own variations - from potato-filled versions in North India to meat-filled ones in Muslim communities, and even sweet versions in some parts. They're often served during festivals, weddings, and special occasions.
